Some Activities to Try

Engaging in Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) activities holds profound value both in professional endeavors and personal growth. In the professional realm, embracing DEI fosters innovation by bringing diverse perspectives to the table, enriching problem-solving processes, and enhancing creativity. Moreover, it cultivates a workplace culture where all individuals feel valued, respected, and empowered to contribute their unique talents and insights. Beyond the office walls, practicing DEI principles nurtures empathy, understanding, and mutual respect in personal interactions, leading to more harmonious relationships and a more inclusive society. By championing DEI initiatives, individuals not only advance their careers but also contribute to creating a world where everyone has the opportunity to thrive, irrespective of their background or identity.

Workplace Activities

Preventing Organizational Biases

The truth is, many organizational-level biases go under the radar even though they are crucial for fostering a fair and inclusive workplace. These questions aim to uncover potential biases in hiring processes, representation in company materials, and pay equity. Addressing these areas may help you discover improvements to create a more equitable and transparent organization.

Consider the organizational-level biases that your organizations may be displaying, whether intentionally or unintentionally.

1) How does your organization fill its positions? Are there company requirements about posting jobs? How might your organization’s processes give rise to biases from social connections in the workforce that you hire? What formal procedures do you think might be adopted to avoid these biases?

2) How does your organization currently depict people on its webpages, materials, and advertisements? Does the diversity that appears reflect the diversity that actually exists in the company? What could be done to better showcase your organization?

3) How does your organization ensure that there are not gender or racial inequities in pay? Is there pay transparency? If not, do they conduct salary studies within their organization? How might you encourage them to do this?

Preventing Conflict

Working in diverse groups has great benefits, but it's true that conflicts and inequity often arises too. Use the questions below to plan how you and your co-workers can prevent these challenges in your own workplace.

1) Have you ever had problems working with co-workers who are different from you? How were they different? What sort of problems arose from these differences?

2) What other downsides to diversity have you experienced or witnessed in your organization?

3) Can you think of any way in which the downsides might have been avoided? Are there strategies that you think the organizations might have adopted to eliminate the problems associated with differences?

Attracting & Recruiting Diverse Employees

How do you make your company somewhere where underrepresented groups and minorities feel eager to apply? Reflecting on your current or recent workplace, consider how recruitment practices, existing policies, and employee attraction strategies impact diversity and inclusion. Evaluate where your organization typically recruits and explore ways to enhance these efforts to attract a more diverse applicant pool.

Reflect on your current (or most recent) workplace and consider these questions:

1) What are the typical places that your organization recruits new applicants? How might you alter or enhance recruiting efforts to get a more diverse set of applicants?

2) What policies does your organization currently have that are appealing to applicants? Are there ones that are missing and badly needed? How might you advocate for these?

3) Which employee attraction strategies do you think could be implemented that are realistic in the context of this organization?




Personal Reflections

It's a good idea to think about how you can live up to the principles of DEI in your personal life. Take some time to reflect on how your identity fits into questions of fairness and inclusion, and how your actions (or inaction) may affect the attitudes and abilities of those around you. You can use the personal reflection activities below to figure out where your place and purpose in the DEI initiative lies.

Activity 1

Foundations of Diversity in Your Personal Life


1) Reflect on your family and friends and consider these questions:
a) Is your extended family more diverse than it was one or two generations ago?
b) How so?
c) In what ways have you personally witnessed the changing demographics in your friendship groups, neighborhood, and other social groups?
2) Think about your experiences at work and consider these questions:
a) How do you personally feel about differences or working with people who are different from you?
b) Do you notice others’ differences?
3) Consider your views on diversity more broadly:
a) What are they?
b) Do you think reflecting on your views about diversity can help you work with others who are different?
c) How might this help?

Activity 2

Reflection: What's Your Narrative?


After you have engaged with the video above, reflect on how you experience the dangers of a single story in your own life. Specifically, consider your overall reflections in response to the following questions:
a) What single story do you think people may have of you?
b) Which strategies have you used to reduce that single narrative?
c) Do you think it is challenging to employ these strategies to reduce that single narrative?
d) How do you think others could support you in your efforts?

Activity 3

Tools for Contempleting Biases in your Life


1) Consider the biases that you or someone you care about are experiencing at work. How are you or your loved one dealing with them at present? What would be ideal? What are the advantages and disadvantages of acting how you would ideally like to act? What is standing in your way?

2) Face the experience by directly naming it and acknowledging feelings. Consider sharing this with the perpetrator. If this is too threatening and/or you fear retaliations, share the experience with a family member, friend, co-worker, or counselor.

3) Understand your and your loved ones’ rights. Many of these can be found here. Learn the steps that one needs to take when reporting discrimination within your organization.

4) Consider keeping a journal to document not only the discriminatory behavior, but also feelings about the transgressions. Ignoring discrimination can often be unhealthy. Name it and use journaling to try to make sense of strategies that can be used to eliminate it and to healthily interpret the problem and your reactions.
